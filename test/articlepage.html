#FutureHype: Imagining The ‘MegaCities’ Of Tomorrow

by Jordan Gold | 10 mins ago
facebookfacebook
twittertwitter
0 Shares

We are living in the “information age”, whatever that means. Between the birth of humanity and the year 2003 five exabytes of information were recorded by humans. Now, in 2015, we humans record more than five exabytes of new information every two days.

What we think we know today may not be at all certain tomorrow, but one thing is for sure: we’re living in the future. So that you can be ready for whatever the future holds, each week we’ll search high and low online to bring you the best futuristic content from the worlds of science, art, design, architecture, computing and robotics.

Welcome, to #Futurehype. This week, Megacities.

The home of tomorrow, today

The term ‘megacity’ was coined back in the 1800s when only 3% of the world’s population actually lived in urban environments. This figure rose dramatically to 47% by the end of the twentieth century because the industrial revolution had made mass city living both possible and necessary for large umbers of people.

As of this year, there are 35 urban conurbations on earth that could be legitimately classed as ‘Megacities’, each of them having a population in excess of 20 million inhabitants. The largest of these are the metropolitan areas of Tokyo, Delhi, Seoul, Shanghai and Mumbai. By the year 2020 however, it is estimated that a further four cities will have achieved ‘Megacity’ status and the world population will be bottoming out somewhere around 7,716,749,000.

Just as the generations before us moved from the quiet countryside to the ever-expanding smog of the cities, so we will be forced to move slowly upwards into high-rises and skyscrapers. You should get used to the brutalist architecture now, it’s inevitably here to stay. But with a bigger population comes more problematic questions; how will the cities of the future cope will the strain on infrastructure? Will future engineers and architects make life easier for us all? Or will technology obliterate us?

Here are some possible features of tomorrow’s MegaCities. Don’t say we didn’t warn you:

Omnidirectional elevators

The MegaCity of the future will likely be composed of towering structures taller and wider than those we are familiar with today. This sheer scale of the buildings we will inhabit poses a serious challenge to the designers looking for a ‘quick fix’.

One such approach to moving people vertically and horizontally is the omnidirectional elevator. Leading inventors in this field are the German conceptual design house ThyssenKrupp with their revolutionary patented MULTI design. The concept is powered by maglev technology, more on that here.

Concept designs by MULTI (c) ThyssenKrupp AG

Driverless living spaces

If Google’s plan to rid the roads of human drivers takes effect then we can expect a radical rethink of how we utilise the interiors of our cars. Cars are surprisingly spacious when you remove the interior seating, steering wheel and driving components. Unveiled by Mercedes-Benz at the CES show in Las Vegas recently, a new model of automobile shows you just how cosy the journey to work could be if you don’t have to drive.

Features include a 360-degree swivelling seat function for conversing and viewing, individual touchscreen displays and interactive gesture-based interfaces for driving and recreating alike. Dieter Zetsche, a big man at Mercedes notes that “Anyone who focuses solely on the technology has not yet grasped how autonomous driving will change our society. The car is growing beyond its role as a mere means of transport and will ultimately become a mobile living space.”

Concept (c) Mercedes Benz from CES Vegas Launch

Power that grows on trees

Technology and aesthetics are often seen as mutually exclusive but it doesn’t have to be that way. New Wind is a project developed by engineer and artist Jérôme Michaud-Larivière to harness nature’s more gentle winds using 72 artificial “leaves” (micro-turbines spinning on a vertical axis) to generate power from the air.

Described as, “Part public art project and part civic infrastructure” the idea behind New Wind is to roll the ‘trees’ out to as many selected areas as possible over the coming years. At just over $36,000 USD the trees don’t come cheap. A micro prototype has already been deployed in Paris with hopes for many more small scale versions to be issued in the near future.

Concept (c) New Wind Technologies

Non stop high-speed train travel

You may remember that we recently wrote about London’s new underground train system which is due to begin on the tube network by the end of this year. These new designs are the work of Priestmangoode, a German design firm who have other, more ambition plans too. There latest pet project is the design and development of a high speed train network that will be able to pick up passengers without stopping.
